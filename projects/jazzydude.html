JazzyDude: A PSoC Digital Synthesizer

JazzyDude was my final project for MIT’s Microcomputer Class (6.115/6.2060). In this class we used an 8051 microcontroller, assembly, and circuits to build classic embedded-systems projects from scratch—ballast-lamp striker, lasers, a robot arm, etc. For the final, we switched to a Cypress PSoC 5LP and I built a jazz-focused instrument synthesizer: you can either play a sax-like tone I reconstructed (and a piano at a much lower sample rate) or have the synthesizer play pre-made standards that can be transposed to any key and BPM.

The first part was simply getting sound out—i.e., the circuit—buffering and routing into the audio amplifier and then to the jack. Next came the waveform itself: a quick Python script captured a 2 s sax note and produced a wavetable. I implemented Direct Digital Synthesis (DDS) to step through that table. I couldn’t use the built-in PSoC wavetable block because I needed amplitude modulation for ADSR and the built-in path wants to wire directly into a DAC, which made modulation on the analog signal awkward.

After that it was lots of parameter tuning to make 16th notes clean (sample rate and an overly long decay cost a couple of days). I wrote C helpers to schedule notes, bars, and full charts so the wavetable player could perform prewritten tunes (encoded as 2D arrays of note structs). Finally, some hardware polish: potentiometers to set BPM and transpose the key.
